# Activity 4 — Submit Prototype Evidence Pack

**Phase:** 2 — Ideation Sprint  
**Type:** Exit (run once, after Proceed gate decision)  
**Estimated time:** 30 minutes  
**Prerequisite:** Sprint Step D completed with Proceed to Phase 3 gate decision  
**Output:** Prototype Evidence Pack — summative artifact for product, mentor, and team review

---

## Purpose

Finalize the Phase 2 artifact and make the final decision explicit.

The Evidence Pack is not a summary of the last sprint cycle. It is a synthesis across all cycles — showing the learning arc from the first hypothesis to the final decision.

This activity forces teams to articulate what they learned, not just what they built.

---

## Learning Objective

By the end of this activity, a team should have:

- a complete record of their ideation and testing process across all cycles
- a synthesis that shows how their understanding evolved
- a clear, evidence-backed Proceed to Phase 3 decision
- a concrete statement of what they will build first in Phase 3

---

## Instructions for Participants

The Evidence Pack has five required sections. Work through them in order.

---

### Section 1: Idea Shortlist and Selection

**What to include:**

- The full idea shortlist from Activity 2 (already stored — just review and confirm)
- The selected idea
- The rationale for choosing this idea over the runner-up (from Activity 3)

**What makes this section strong:**

- The rejected ideas are named and the rejection reasoning is honest
- The selected idea is clearly connected to the Phase 1 leverage point
- The rationale shows that the choice was deliberate, not arbitrary

---

### Section 2: Sprint Log

One entry per cycle. The sprint log is the spine of the Evidence Pack. It shows the learning arc.

Each entry includes:

```
Cycle [N]:
  Hypothesis: [the specific prediction this cycle tested]
  Method: [prototype method used]
  Prototype: [brief description or link to artifact]
  Users tested: [number and description]
  Key finding: [the most important thing observed — behavioral]
  Gate decision: [Refine / Pivot / Proceed / Kill chosen at the end of this cycle]
  What changed: [what this cycle revealed that changed the team's understanding]
```

**What makes this section strong:**

- Each cycle's hypothesis is meaningfully different from the previous one (shows learning)
- Key findings are behavioral, not opinion-based
- The "what changed" field shows that the team actually updated their understanding — not just executed steps

**Common weakness:**

Teams copy the same hypothesis across all cycles and change only superficial details. If cycles 1, 2, and 3 all tested the same thing in slightly different wrappers, the sprint log will show it. This significantly weakens the Evidence Pack.

---

### Section 3: Final Prototype

The artifact from the last sprint cycle.

- photo, link, or written description
- brief note on what this prototype was designed to test (the final hypothesis)
- brief note on what changed between this prototype and the first cycle's prototype

**What makes this section strong:**

- The evolution from cycle 1 to the final prototype is visible
- The final prototype is clearly more targeted than the first (narrower, sharper, closer to the core assumption)

---

### Section 4: Feedback Synthesis

A synthesis across all cycles — not a per-cycle summary.

Answer these questions:

**4A. What worked consistently across all cycles?**

Name behaviors or reactions that appeared in multiple cycles, across different users, regardless of which prototype version they saw. These are strong signals.

**4B. What consistently did not work?**

Name behaviors or reactions that were consistently negative or absent across cycles. These are signals too — they point to assumptions that the evidence broke.

**4C. How did the core assumption evolve?**

The core assumption from Activity 3 — how accurate was it? Did the evidence support it, break it, or reveal that it was the wrong assumption to test?

**4D. What was the most influential finding across all cycles?**

The single observation, pattern, or surprise that most shaped the final decision.

**What makes this section strong:**

- Named patterns are traced across multiple cycles, not just one
- The synthesis is honest about contradictions (not everything will point the same direction)
- The most influential finding is specific — a behavior, a quote, a moment from a session

---

### Section 5: Final Decision and Phase 3 Plan

**5A. Decision**

Proceed to Phase 3.

State in one sentence why this decision is justified: what evidence, across what cycles, supports it?

**5B. What you are building first**

One sentence: the first thing to build in Phase 3, based on what the prototype and testing revealed.

This is not a full product roadmap. It is the first move.

> "We will build [specific feature or experience] first because [evidence from testing] showed that [behavior], and that is the highest-leverage place to start."

**5C. What would change the decision**

Name the evidence that would make you reverse the Proceed decision and Kill.

This is not a formality. It is a check on the quality of your reasoning. If you cannot name what would change your mind, you have not been honest about the uncertainty in your evidence.

---

## Grading

### Completion check (pass/fail)

All five sections must be submitted with substantive responses. Empty or placeholder sections fail.

| Section | Pass condition |
|---|---|
| 1: Idea Shortlist | Shortlist shown, selected idea named, rationale present |
| 2: Sprint Log | One complete entry per cycle run |
| 3: Final Prototype | Artifact present and described |
| 4: Feedback Synthesis | All four synthesis questions answered |
| 5: Final Decision | Decision stated, rationale present, first Phase 3 move named |

### Phase-level rubric

This is the summative rubric for the entire phase. Applied by the mentor after the Evidence Pack is submitted.

#### Dimension 1: Idea grounding (max 3)

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 0 | No shortlist or rationale |
| 1 | Shortlist exists but ideas are variations of one concept |
| 2 | Multiple distinct ideas; selected idea connected to Phase 1 evidence |
| 3 | Multiple distinct ideas; selected idea is clearly the best bet given Phase 1 evidence; rejection reasoning is honest and specific |

#### Dimension 2: Loop honesty (max 3)

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 0 | Only one cycle, or sprint log not completed |
| 1 | Multiple cycles but each tests essentially the same hypothesis |
| 2 | Each cycle tests a distinct hypothesis that evolved from the prior cycle's findings |
| 3 | Sprint log shows a clear learning arc — the team can articulate how their understanding changed cycle by cycle |

#### Dimension 3: Evidence quality (max 3)

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 0 | No test evidence, or evidence from teammates only |
| 1 | Evidence is opinion-based ("users liked it") with no behavioral detail |
| 2 | Evidence is behavioral, from real users, with at least one surprise finding across cycles |
| 3 | Evidence across multiple cycles shows consistent patterns or honest contradictions. Most influential finding is specific and behavioral. |

#### Dimension 4: Decision quality (max 3)

| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| 0 | No decision or decision with no rationale |
| 1 | Decision stated without connection to evidence |
| 2 | Decision supported by specific evidence from the sprint log |
| 3 | Decision integrates evidence across all cycles, names the most influential finding, names what would reverse the decision, and names the first Phase 3 move with a clear evidence-backed rationale |

**Phase-level total: 12 points.**

**Score to outcome:**

| Score | Outcome |
|---|---|
| 10–12 | Excellent — strong evidence base, ready for Phase 3 |
| 7–9 | Solid — proceed with mentor guidance |
| 4–6 | Developing — recommend additional cycle before Phase 3 |
| 0–3 | Not yet — revisit testing process |

## Mentor Review

After the Evidence Pack is submitted, mentors conduct a final review. This is the most important mentor touchpoint in Phase 2.

### What to assess

**Learning arc:** Does the sprint log show genuine learning? Can you see the team's understanding evolve cycle by cycle?

**Evidence honesty:** Does the synthesis acknowledge what didn't work, or does it only present supporting evidence? Cherry-picked evidence is a red flag.

**Decision confidence:** Is the Proceed decision calibrated to the evidence, or is it premature? A team with one supported cycle and two inconclusive ones may need more evidence before Phase 3.

**First Phase 3 move:** Is the first thing they plan to build clearly derived from what they learned in testing? Or does it feel like the original idea, unaffected by the evidence?

### Mentor actions

After reviewing the Evidence Pack:

- **Approve:** team is ready for Phase 3
- **Approve with notes:** team proceeds, but mentor leaves feedback they should read first
- **Request revision:** one specific section needs to be strengthened before approval. Mentor names the section and the standard it should meet.
- **Recommend one more sprint cycle:** evidence is present but thin. Team should run one more cycle before Phase 3.

Mentor response target: within 2 hours of submission in hackathon context.

---

## Final Phase Grade

After mentor review, the phase grade is computed.

| Component | Weight | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Step completion (all pass/fail checks) | Required — gates submission | Each step |
| Average cycle rubric score | 50% | Sprint log entries, mentor-assessed |
| Evidence Pack rubric score | 50% | This activity, mentor-assessed |

**Final score: 0–100.**

The grade is shown to the team and recorded. It is advisory in a hackathon context — mentors retain discretion to advance teams based on judgment even if the score is borderline.

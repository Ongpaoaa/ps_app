## 1. Executive Summary

Phase 2 begins the moment a team has a Problem Proof Pack.

They have evidence. They have a leverage point. They have a Proceed decision.

Now they need to figure out **what to build** — and whether it actually works before they build it.

This phase uses the same backward design logic as Phase 1:

1. define what teams must walk away with
2. define what evidence proves they tested a real idea
3. build only the activities required to produce that evidence

**The structure is not linear. It has a loop.**

```
LINEAR ENTRY (run once)
  Activity 1 — Orientation: What You'll Walk Away With
  Activity 2 — Generate Many Ideas, Judge None Yet
  Activity 3 — Select Your Best Bet

  [PROTOTYPE SPRINT LOOP — run 1 to 3 times]
  Sprint Step A — Choose Your Prototype Method
  Sprint Step B — Build the Prototype
  Sprint Step C — Test With Real Users
  Sprint Step D — Synthesize + Gate

  EXIT (when team gates out with Proceed or Kill)
  Activity 4 — Submit Prototype Evidence Pack
```

Each pass through the Sprint Loop produces evidence. The gate at the end of each loop asks: do you have enough signal to proceed to Phase 3, or do you need another cycle?

The end state:

**Prototype Evidence Pack + Proceed to Phase 3 / Kill decision**

Everything in this phase builds toward that.

---

## 2. Why This Phase Exists

### What teams get wrong after Phase 1

Most teams leave problem discovery and immediately:

- lock onto the first idea they brainstorm
- spend days building code before testing anything
- assume the idea is good because the problem was real

### Core decision

A real problem does not guarantee a good solution. Teams must test the idea before they trust it.

The method: low-fidelity prototyping inside a repeatable sprint loop. Not code. Not design polish. The cheapest possible version of the experience — in front of real users — to find out what works.

### Why the loop matters

A single prototype-test cycle is rarely enough. The first test tells you what's wrong. The second tells you whether you fixed the right thing. The third confirms the signal.

The loop is not busywork. Each iteration is a bet: "I think the problem is X, and I think this prototype will prove or break that." The gate forces the team to articulate what they learned and what they're betting next.

A team that runs two tight loops is almost always in a better position than a team that runs one polished one.

---

## 3. Backward Design Principle

Same logic as Phase 1:

1. **Desired results** — what should teams understand and be able to do?
2. **Acceptable evidence** — what artifacts prove they tested a real idea with real users?
3. **Learning plan** — what activities and loops produce that evidence?

### Design rule

If an activity or loop step does not move the team closer to a tested prototype with real user feedback, cut it, merge it, or make it optional.

---

## 4. Stage 1: Desired Results

### Transfer goal

By the end of Phase 2, a team should be able to say:

> We generated multiple ideas, selected the most promising one, ran at least one prototype-test cycle with real users, and know whether to build it or kill it.

### End-of-phase capability

A successful team can:

- generate a range of ideas instead of anchoring on the first one
- select an idea based on Phase 1 evidence, not gut feel
- match a prototype method to their solution type
- build a testable artifact in under 2 hours
- run a real test with real users and synthesize what they learned
- articulate what they learned from each loop iteration
- make a reasoned Proceed to Phase 3 / Kill decision

### Enduring understandings

Teams should leave Phase 2 understanding that:

- a real problem does not guarantee that any solution will work
- testing a prototype is faster than debugging built code
- most first ideas are wrong — the goal is to find out fast
- a second loop with a sharper hypothesis beats a polished first prototype
- a prototype that kills a bad idea has saved the team days of wasted work

### Essential questions

The phase should keep returning to these questions:

1. What is the simplest possible version of this idea we could put in front of a user?
2. What specific hypothesis are we testing with this prototype?
3. What did users actually do, not just what they said?
4. What did we learn, and does it change what we build next?
5. Do we have enough signal to proceed, or do we need another cycle?

## 5. Stage 2: Acceptable Evidence

Phase 2 is successful only if teams leave with a **Prototype Evidence Pack** containing:

1. **Idea shortlist** — ideas considered and why they chose this one
2. **Sprint log** — one entry per loop iteration: method used, prototype built, users tested, what they learned
3. **Final prototype** — the artifact from the last sprint cycle
4. **Feedback synthesis** — patterns across all cycles
5. **Decision** — Proceed to Phase 3 or Kill, with reasoning

### Evidence standard

The final pack should prove four things:

#### A. Ideation quality

Teams can:

- list multiple distinct ideas (not variations of one idea)
- connect their chosen idea to Phase 1 evidence
- explain why they did not choose the other ideas

#### B. Loop quality

Teams can:

- show at least one complete prototype-test cycle
- show that the second cycle (if run) was different from the first in a specific, testable way
- explain what they changed between cycles and why

#### C. Test evidence quality

Teams can:

- test with real users (not just teammates) in each cycle
- capture behavior — what users did, where they got confused, what they skipped
- surface at least one surprising finding per cycle

#### D. Decision quality

Teams can:

- choose Proceed to Phase 3 or Kill
- justify the decision with cumulative test evidence across all cycles
- name the hypothesis they started with and whether the evidence supported it

### Minimum rubric for mentors and reviewers

1. **Idea grounding** — is the chosen idea traceable to Phase 1 evidence?
2. **Loop honesty** — did each cycle test a specific hypothesis, or was it the same test repeated?
3. **Test quality** — did real users interact with real artifacts?
4. **Decision quality** — is the decision earned by evidence across cycles, not just the last one?

## 6. Stage 3: Learning Plan

Phase 2 has **3 linear entry activities + 1 repeatable sprint loop (4 steps) + 1 exit activity**.

---

### Activity 1 — What You'll Walk Away With

**Purpose**  
Set the promise. Frame Phase 2 as a loop, not a checklist.

**Backward-design role**  
Show the end state first. Teams should not leave thinking the goal is to have a clever idea or finish the steps. The goal is to have evidence from real tests.

**Key messages**

- You already found a real problem. Now find out whether your solution idea actually works — before you build anything.
- This phase has a loop. You prototype, test, learn, and decide whether to loop again or exit.
- One loop is the minimum. Two loops is better. The loop is not failure — it is the method.

**Re-entry note for Pivot teams**  
Teams who Pivoted in Phase 1 enter here with a revised problem framing, not a clean one. They should treat their revised framing as their starting hypothesis and move directly to Activity 2. Their Phase 1 Problem Proof Pack is still the evidence base.

**Format**  
Short webtoon / visual intro showing the loop structure.

**Output**  
Orientation only. No graded artifact.

---

### Activity 2 — Generate Many Ideas, Judge None Yet

**Purpose**  
Break teams out of first-idea anchoring.

**Backward-design role**  
An idea shortlist only has value if there were real alternatives. Teams who anchor early never know whether they chose well.

**Teams should generate ideas that:**

- address the leverage point they found in Phase 1
- are realistic enough to prototype in under 2 hours
- vary in approach — not just variations of the same solution

**Constraints:**

- minimum 5 distinct ideas before evaluation begins
- no criticism or judgment during generation
- ideas must trace to Phase 1 evidence

**Format**  
Guided ideation exercise. Timer-based team activity.

**Output**  
Idea shortlist with brief description of each.

**Evidence value**  
Confirms teams are not anchoring on the first idea.

---

### Activity 3 — Select Your Best Bet

**Purpose**  
Force a reasoned selection. Not a vote by gut.

**Backward-design role**  
The shortlist has no value unless teams can explain why they chose one idea over the others.

**Selection criteria teams must consider:**

- does this idea address the leverage point from Phase 1?
- can we build a prototype and test it before the day ends?
- do we have access to users who would actually experience this problem?
- what is the single biggest assumption this idea depends on?

**Output**  
One selected idea with written rationale connecting to Phase 1 evidence, plus one named hypothesis: "We believe [target user] will [do/feel/respond in this way] because [Phase 1 finding]."

**Evidence value**  
Confirms the choice is evidence-grounded and hypothesis-driven, not arbitrary.

---

### [PROTOTYPE SPRINT LOOP]

Teams run this loop 1 to 3 times. Each iteration is one sprint cycle.

The minimum is one complete cycle. Two cycles is the target for most hackathon teams.

At the end of each cycle, the Gate determines whether to loop again or exit.

---

#### Sprint Step A — Choose Your Prototype Method

**Purpose**  
Match the method to what is being tested.

**Backward-design role**  
Teams who skip this step default to Figma for everything, including ideas that need Wizard of Oz or Concierge MVP.

**In the first cycle:** choose the fastest method that can test the core hypothesis.

**In subsequent cycles:** you may use a different method if the hypothesis changed.

---

**The five methods:**

**Paper Prototype**
- Draw screens on paper. Flip between them manually during user testing.
- Best for: apps, dashboards, flows.
- Time to build: 1-2 hours.
- Test: sit with user, ask them to "tap" the paper.

**Wizard of Oz**
- A team member secretly operates the "magic" while the user thinks it's real.
- Example: user types into a chatbot — a human on another tab types the response.
- Best for: AI features, complex logic, personalization.
- Reveals: does the experience work, before building the system.

**Figma / Slides Clickthrough**
- Static screens linked together. No real logic, just navigation.
- Best for: sharing remotely or testing multiple users.
- Time to build: 2-4 hours.

**Concierge MVP**
- Do the service manually for real users — no automation.
- Example: instead of building a matching algorithm, a team member manually matches people via DM.
- Best for: marketplace, matching, curation features.
- Reveals: does anyone actually want this?

**Storyboard / Role Play**
- Sketch the user journey as a comic strip, or act it out physically.
- Best for: physical products, service design, multi-step experiences.
- Fastest to produce. Great for team alignment.

**Method selection guide:**

| Situation | Method |
|---|---|
| Testing a screen flow | Paper or Figma |
| Testing an AI/smart feature | Wizard of Oz |
| Testing if the service has demand | Concierge MVP |
| Testing a multi-step experience | Storyboard / Role play |
| Need to test remotely | Figma clickthrough |

**Output**  
Named prototype method + one-sentence explanation of why it matches the hypothesis being tested this cycle.

---

#### Sprint Step B — Build the Prototype

**Purpose**  
Create a testable artifact — fast.

**Backward-design role**  
A prototype that is not built cannot be tested. Bias strongly toward speed over polish.

**Build constraints:**

- completable within 2 hours maximum
- testable by a user who has no prior context
- exposes only what the current hypothesis requires

**Teams should not:**

- spend more than 2 hours building
- add features the test does not need
- polish the visual design instead of testing the logic

**Evidence submission per cycle:**

- paper prototype: photo upload
- Wizard of Oz: written setup description + test script
- Figma: share link
- Concierge MVP: written process description
- Storyboard: photo upload

**Output**  
Prototype artifact for this cycle.

---

#### Sprint Step C — Test With Real Users

**Purpose**  
Gather behavioral evidence from real people interacting with the prototype.

**Backward-design role**  
Test results are the only evidence that tells teams whether their hypothesis holds. Opinions about the idea are not evidence.

**How to find users in a hackathon:**

- Start with the target user from Phase 1 — do you know anyone who fits?
- Hallway testing: approach people outside your team who match the profile
- Teammate-of-teammate rule: your teammates' friends or family can work if they genuinely fit the target user
- Mentor-as-user fallback: use a mentor only if they have real experience with the problem — not as a substitute for a real user

**Test protocol:**

- 3 users minimum per cycle
- do not explain the idea before the test — just give a task and watch
- do not coach, correct, or narrate during the test
- capture: what they did, where they paused, what confused them, what they said without prompting
- note the one thing that surprised you most

**Output**  
Test notes from 3+ sessions per cycle. Photos, typed notes, or audio summaries.

**Evidence value**  
Primary raw dataset for the decision gate.

---

#### Sprint Step D — Synthesize + Gate

**Purpose**  
Turn raw test notes into a learning, then decide whether to loop again or exit.

**Backward-design role**  
The gate forces teams to articulate what they learned and whether it changes what they build next. Without this step, teams accumulate observations without synthesizing them.

**Synthesis questions (answer before the gate):**

1. What did we set out to test this cycle?
2. What specifically happened — what did users do?
3. What was the most surprising finding?
4. What does this tell us about our hypothesis?
5. What would we test differently next cycle if we ran one?

**Gate options:**

- **Loop again — Refine:** evidence suggests the idea has signal but the hypothesis needs sharpening. State the new hypothesis before starting the next cycle. You must be able to name what is different about the next test.
- **Loop again — Pivot concept:** this idea is wrong but the problem is real. Return to Activity 3 and select a different idea. Bring what you learned into the next selection.
- **Proceed to Phase 3:** strong signal across cycles. The hypothesis held. You know enough to move to building.
- **Kill:** weak signal or the evidence suggests the problem framing from Phase 1 was wrong. Return to Phase 1 or exit the program.

**Loop limit:** After 3 complete cycles, teams must exit — Proceed or Kill. Three loops is enough evidence for any decision.

**Output**  
Sprint log entry: synthesis answers + gate decision + (if looping) the new hypothesis for the next cycle.

---

### Activity 4 — Submit Prototype Evidence Pack

**Purpose**  
Finalize the Phase 2 artifact.

**Backward-design role**  
Collect everything and force teams to make a final judgment call based on cumulative evidence across all cycles.

**Required contents:**

1. **Idea Shortlist**
   - ideas generated
   - selected idea
   - why this one, why not the others

2. **Sprint Log** (one entry per cycle)
   - cycle number
   - hypothesis tested
   - prototype method and artifact
   - users tested
   - key finding
   - gate decision

3. **Final Prototype**
   - artifact from the last cycle (photo / link / documented description)

4. **Feedback Synthesis**
   - what consistently worked across cycles
   - what consistently did not
   - the assumption that changed most as cycles progressed

5. **Decision**
   - Proceed to Phase 3 or Kill
   - why
   - what evidence across cycles most influenced the decision

**Output**  
Prototype Evidence Pack.

**Evidence value**  
Summative artifact for product, mentor, and team review.

## 7. Pedagogical Guardrails

### What this phase should teach

- how to generate multiple ideas before committing to one
- how to match a prototype method to what is being tested
- how to form a hypothesis before each test — not just "let's see what happens"
- how to read behavior, not just hear opinions
- how to use each cycle's learning to sharpen the next
- how to make a reasoned exit decision when evidence is cumulative and sometimes contradictory

### What this phase should not teach

- that the first prototype-test cycle gives you a definitive answer
- that a second or third loop is failure
- that user positivity means the idea works
- that teams must build because they already invested time in prototyping
- that finishing the loop is the same as having evidence

### Pedagogical stance

This phase should feel like:

- fast and iterative
- hypothesis-driven
- slightly demanding
- honest about uncertainty
- oriented toward learning, not completion

It should not feel like:

- design theater
- startup pitch culture
- "run through the checklist and you're done"
- a single-pass exercise with a predetermined answer

## 8. Feedback Loop Design

Every step inside the Sprint Loop (and Activity 3) has a very short feedback loop: AI feedback fires immediately on submission, and human feedback arrives async.

### Why this exists

Without feedback at each step, teams move through the loop mechanically. The AI feedback is a forcing function — it surfaces blind spots before they compound into a bad gate decision. The human feedback (mentor or peer) adds a second signal that AI cannot provide: judgment from someone who knows the domain or knows the team.

### Design principles

- **AI feedback is always immediate and non-blocking.** It appears within 60 seconds of submission. Teams do not wait for it — it arrives as they read the next step.
- **Human feedback is always async and non-blocking.** Teams proceed. The feedback appears when it arrives. No step is gated on a mentor responding.
- **The one soft exception:** Sprint Step D (Gate) can display a "waiting for mentor acknowledgment" indicator before allowing Proceed to Phase 3. This is a soft nudge, not a hard lock. If no mentor responds within 30 minutes, the gate auto-opens.
- **Feedback is visible to the whole team and to the mentor.** Not private.
- **AI does not score.** It asks questions and names patterns. Scoring would shift team focus to the grade, not the evidence.

### Feedback behavior per step

| Step | AI feedback | Human feedback |
|---|---|---|
| Activity 3 — Select + Hypothesis | Checks: is the hypothesis specific? Is it testable with a prototype? Surfaces one clarifying question if vague. | Mentor can comment before team enters the sprint. Non-blocking. |
| Sprint Step A — Choose Method | Checks: does the method match the hypothesis? If mismatch (e.g., chose Figma for an AI feature), names the concern. | Mentor can suggest a different method. Non-blocking. |
| Sprint Step B — Build Prototype | Checks: is an artifact submitted? Is the description enough for a user to interact without coaching? Flags if test-readiness is unclear. | Mentor can preview the artifact and leave a note. Non-blocking. |
| Sprint Step C — Test Notes | Synthesizes 2-3 patterns from the notes. Flags if notes contain only opinions (no behavior). Asks: "What surprised you?" if not mentioned. | Mentor can highlight a finding or add an observation. Non-blocking. |
| Sprint Step D — Gate | Validates the synthesis: does the evidence support the gate decision? Flags if the team is Proceeding with thin evidence or Killing with strong evidence. | Mentor acknowledges or comments on the gate decision. Soft gate for Proceed to Phase 3 (30-min auto-open). |

### What AI feedback looks like in the UI

After each submission, a feedback card appears below the confirmation. It contains:

- 1-2 observations about what was submitted
- 1 question the team should be able to answer before moving on

Example after Sprint Step C (Test Notes):

> "Your notes mention users paused at the pricing step three times. That's a pattern worth naming. One thing not mentioned: what did users do after they paused? Did they proceed, abandon, or ask for help? That behavior matters more than the pause itself."
> "Can you answer this before your synthesis: what would you need to see in the next test to feel confident about your gate decision?"

### What human feedback looks like in the UI

A comment thread on each step — visible to team and mentor. Mentors can:

- leave a text comment
- leave a thumbs-up (quick signal: "looks good, keep going")
- flag for attention (signals the team should read this before proceeding)

Teams can reply to mentor comments within the thread.

## 9. Loop Structure for the App

### How to represent the loop in the mobile activity engine

The Sprint Loop (Steps A-D) is a repeating unit. Each pass is one sprint cycle.

**Recommended implementation:**

- Activities 1-3 are fixed linear activities
- The Sprint Loop is a repeatable block that teams can enter up to 3 times
- Each loop iteration creates a sprint log entry
- The Gate at the end of each iteration determines next state:
  - Loop again (Refine) → re-enter Sprint Step A with new hypothesis pre-filled
  - Loop again (Pivot) → return to Activity 3 (Select Best Bet)
  - Proceed / Kill → go to Activity 4 (Submit Evidence Pack)
- Activity 4 is the exit point

**Minimum viable representation if the engine does not support loops:**

Represent each sprint cycle as a block of 4 sequential activities (A, B, C, D). Pre-build 2 blocks in the seed (Cycle 1 and Cycle 2). Gate at the end of Cycle 1 unlocks Cycle 2 or Activity 4. Gate at the end of Cycle 2 leads to Activity 4.

This is a simpler model but makes the looping intent visible in the UI.

## 9. Technical Product Requirements

### Required content types

- webtoon / intro visual (showing the loop structure explicitly)
- guided ideation worksheet
- idea selection form with hypothesis field
- prototype method explainer with selection guide
- prototype artifact upload (photo / link / description) — per cycle
- user test notes upload — per cycle
- synthesis + gate form — per cycle
- final Prototype Evidence Pack submission

### Feedback layer requirements

For each sprint step that has AI feedback:

- AI feedback is triggered on submission (webhook or server-side event)
- AI feedback response time target: < 60 seconds
- AI feedback is stored and associated with the specific step + cycle
- AI feedback is readable by both team and mentor

For human feedback:

- Comment thread per step, visible to team + mentor
- Mentor actions: comment, thumbs-up, flag for attention
- Team can reply to mentor comments
- Sprint Step D gate: "waiting for mentor" soft indicator with 30-minute auto-open
- Notification to assigned mentor when team submits Sprint Step D with Proceed to Phase 3 decision

### Assessment requirements

At minimum we need per cycle:

1. **Prototype artifact** — photo, link, or description
2. **Test notes** — evidence from 3+ user sessions
3. **Sprint log entry** — synthesis answers + gate decision
4. **AI feedback record** — stored per step per cycle (for mentor visibility and future analysis)

And for the final submission:

5. **Prototype Evidence Pack** — with Proceed to Phase 3 / Kill decision

### Seed structure

The Phase 2 seed should represent:

- 3 fixed linear activities (Orientation, Generate Ideas, Select Best Bet)
- 2 sprint cycle blocks (each with 4 steps: Method, Build, Test, Gate)
- 1 exit activity (Submit Evidence Pack)

Total: 11 activity slots (3 + 4 + 4 + 1), with the second cycle unlocked by the gate in cycle 1.

## 10. What We Are Cutting

The following should not be in Phase 2 core flow:

- separate "design thinking" lecture before ideation begins
- Figma-only prototyping guidance
- user persona recreation (already done in Phase 1)
- lengthy feature planning or product roadmaps
- polish-first prototyping culture
- single-pass prototype-test-decide flow that implies one cycle is enough

## 13. Success Criteria

Phase 2 is successful if:

- teams generated multiple ideas before selecting one
- the selected idea is traceable to Phase 1 evidence
- teams stated a clear hypothesis before each sprint cycle
- teams ran at least 1 full sprint cycle with real (non-teammate) users
- teams can show what changed between cycles (if they ran more than one)
- the Proceed to Phase 3 / Kill decision is justified by cumulative evidence, not just the last test
- mentors can assess whether the team learned across cycles or just executed steps
